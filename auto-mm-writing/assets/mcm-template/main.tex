\begin{abstract}

    We develop a unified analytical framework for \textit{Dancing with the Stars} that (i) infers latent fan vote shares via constrained inverse optimization with 91\% elimination consistency, (ii) compares rank-based and percentage-based aggregation rules through counterfactual season simulations, (iii) applies a multi-head attention mechanism (QKV) to reveal how judges and fans weight contestant attributes differently, and (iv) proposes a phased hybrid voting policy balancing audience engagement with technical credibility.

    For \textbf{Task 1}, we construct normalized score functions for both the rank-based and percentage-based aggregation rules. By imposing elimination-consistency constraints, we design a loss function and perform gradient descent in the fan-vote direction to recover latent vote shares. Cross-week stability diagnostics ensure that inferred votes remain within realistic bounds---fan support typically evolves smoothly without extreme spikes (``going viral'') or collapses (``falling out of favor'').

    For \textbf{Task 2}, we apply both rules season-by-season to obtain two elimination sets, then take the symmetric difference to measure how each rule respects audience preferences. Within this difference set, we compute normalized fan votes and quantify the proportion of weeks in which the rank rule eliminates contestants with higher audience support than the percentage rule. The analysis shows the percentage rule more frequently retains high-vote contestants, indicating stronger alignment with audience preference. A Season~27 case study highlights the judge--fan tension emphasized in the problem statement.

    For \textbf{Task 3}, we adapt a multi-head attention mechanism (QKV) to simulate how judges and fans weight contestant attributes. We conduct ablation experiments comparing static categorical encoding with semantically enriched numerical encoding, verifying that the attention mechanism captures meaningful patterns. To address limited data, we orthogonally initialize the Query matrix and introduce a head-diversity penalty to encourage differentiated attention heads. We then analyze the $\mathrm{softmax}(QK^\top)$ weights to interpret how each feature contributes to final scores. Despite regularization (weight decay, cosine annealing) and reduced head dimensions, some overfitting risk remains due to the small sample size, so results are interpreted as statistical tendencies rather than causal effects.

    For \textbf{Task 4}, our analysis indicates that the percentage rule better reflects audience preference, but audience subjectivity intensifies as the competition progresses---late-season emotional voting becomes more pronounced. We therefore propose a hybrid policy: retain percentage-based aggregation in early weeks to honor fan engagement, and introduce a judge-selected bottom-three elimination starting at mid-season (Week~7 in a 14-week season, or generally the first week after the midpoint) to restore balance between professional assessment and audience sentiment.

    \vspace{5pt}
    \textbf{Keywords: DWTS; Fan Vote Inference; Rank vs Percentage; Judge-Save; Attention Model; Hybrid Policy}

\end{abstract}
